ANIMAL CELLS CAN TOUCH AND TALK TO ONE OTHER

by bensonshaji . 05 Jan 2014    
Tweet

Scientist have discovered that animal cells can communicate with one another by making physical contact to one another. This mechanism is similar to the way neurons communicate with other cells by splitting out signalling proteins into extra-cellular fluid and hoping to find the right target says senior investigator Thomas B. Kornberg, professor of biochemistry with university of california, San Francisco.

Working with living tissue from fruit flies, scientist demonstrated that cells send out long, thin tubes of cytoplasm called cytonemes which can extend across the length of 50 or 100 cells before touching the cells they are targetting. The point of contact between a cytoneme and its target cells acts as a communication bridge between the two cells. This is similar to the transferring of signals by neuron called as synapses, and transmitting the responses over long distances in long tubes called axons. Through this demonstration scientist have shown that many types of animal cells have the same ability to reach out a synapse with one another in order to communicate.
